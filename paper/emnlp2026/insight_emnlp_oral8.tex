\title{\method: A Language-Grounded Risk Concept Interface for Interpretable Accident Anticipation}

\author{Anonymous Submission}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Accident anticipation is usually evaluated as an opaque binary prediction task.
For safety-critical use, however, a strong score is not enough: researchers
also need an auditable semantic interface that exposes what the model treats as
risky and how those semantics affect warning behavior. We present
\method{}, an interpretable anticipation framework built around a governed
language-grounded risk concept interface. The method mines candidate concepts
from driving frames, refines them into canonical risk primitives, balances
semantic families, and exports a trainable ontology with review metadata and
merge provenance. The ontology is then used as the concept bottleneck of an
anticipation model, enabling matched comparisons of how semantic-interface
design changes the AP--mTTA operating point. Across DAD and A3D, \method{}
reaches a strong interpretable operating point, including 93.40\% AP and
4.90s mTTA on A3D and 68.19\% AP on the canonical DAD curriculum line, while
using only about 30M parameters. The paper's strongest claim is semantic
interface methodology: ontology construction is not hidden preprocessing, but a
governed modeling choice whose downstream effects are measurable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Useful accident anticipation systems should do more than produce a final risk
score. In ADAS and autonomous-driving pipelines, the model should expose a
stable intermediate object: a semantic layer that identifies what the system
regards as dangerous, lets alternative risk vocabularies be compared, and
supports audit of how semantics influence warning behavior. Current systems
rarely provide this object. Many strong models optimize AP and timing metrics
while leaving their internal evidence latent; others attach post-hoc
visualization or verbalization after prediction~\citep{ref2,liao2024and}. This
is useful for explanation, but it is not the same as a trainable and governed
semantic interface.

We argue that the missing object is a \emph{language-grounded risk concept
interface}. The interface is not a prompt artifact or a presentation device. It
is a governed ontology that becomes the model's semantic bottleneck and can
therefore be evaluated as part of the method. This shift changes the main
scientific question from ``which black-box model has the best leaderboard
score?'' to ``how does the design of the semantic interface change the
accuracy--timeliness frontier?'' Figure~\ref{fig:motivation} shows the intended
reading: named concepts, temporal evidence, and warning behavior should be
visible together, while the experiments below keep the corresponding
quantitative claims in protocol-separated evidence tiers.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate accident anticipation around an auditable semantic
  interface, treating ontology construction as a modeling choice rather than
  hidden preprocessing.
  \item We introduce a reproducible multimodal concept-construction pipeline
  that mines, refines, merges, balances, and lightly reviews risk concepts into
  a trainable ontology.
  \item We build \method{}, a concept-bottleneck anticipation model that exposes
  concept-level risk evidence while remaining in a compact 30M-parameter regime.
  \item We show that ontology choice changes the AP--mTTA operating point under
  matched launchers on DAD and A3D. We keep DAD fragility and actor-policy
  timing evidence scoped as support, not as the central claim.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Accident anticipation.}
Early and recent accident anticipation methods model pre-crash video as a
prediction problem, combining recurrent, graph, attention, or multimodal
architectures to improve AP and warning time~\citep{ref4,ref17,ref32,liao2024crash}.
Recent systems such as CCAF-Net~\citep{liu2025ccaf} and
LATTE~\citep{ZHANG2025103173} push DAD scores higher, but they do not expose a
trainable semantic state that can be named, compared, and audited across runs.

\paragraph{Explanation and language grounding.}
Post-hoc visual explanations such as Grad-CAM can localize evidence after the
fact, and language-based systems can verbalize accident regions or scene
descriptions~\citep{ref2,liao2024and}. \method{} instead places language-derived
risk concepts inside the forward path. It is therefore closer in spirit to
concept bottlenecks and interpretable representation learning: a constrained
intermediate space is optimized for utility while remaining inspectable
\citep{tishby2000information}. Our contribution is to make the vocabulary itself
governed and experimentally variable in accident anticipation.

\section{Method}

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.98\textwidth]{../figures/emnlp_fig2_framework_image2_overlay.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{\method{} as a governed semantic-interface pipeline.}
  The figure separates video evidence, language-grounded concept governance,
  the trainable \cbm{} state, and downstream warning/audit behavior. Exact
  scientific labels are drawn deterministically; the visual design emphasizes
  that the same named concepts support prediction, timing, and audit.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure*}

Given a dashcam video $\{\mathbf{x}_t\}_{t=1}^T$, accident label $y$, and
accident time $\tau$, standard anticipation predicts whether an accident will
occur from a prefix. We instead optimize for a useful operating region:
high predictive quality with warning lead time and an inspectable semantic
state. The idealized target is
\begin{equation}
  \max_{\theta}\; \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{TTA}]
  \quad \mathrm{s.t.}\quad
  \mathrm{AP}(\theta)\geq \mathrm{AP}_{\text{base}},
\end{equation}
where AP is precision--recall area and TTA is warning lead time. The key
methodological claim is that the semantic interface determines how this
operating point can be studied.

\paragraph{Ontology construction.}
The concept vocabulary is built through five stages. First, we mine pre-crash
frames that cover diverse traffic geometries, actor interactions, visibility
conditions, and precursor patterns. Second, a multimodal model proposes
risk-first concepts rather than generic scene captions. Third, noisy proposals
are refined into reusable risk primitives by removing background clutter,
merging near-duplicates, and normalizing names. Fourth, family-level balancing
and light human review prevent the ontology from collapsing into a small
hand-written list or an ungoverned phrase pool. Fifth, the ontology is exported
with canonical names, family metadata, merge provenance, and review notes.

\paragraph{Concept-bottleneck anticipation.}
Once an ontology is fixed, \method{} maps object-focused frame features to
concept activations $\mathbf{c}_t\in[0,1]^K$ using a \cbm{}:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{split}
  \mathbf{c}_t &= \sigma(\mathbf{W}_c\mathbf{o}_t+\mathbf{b}_c),\\
  \mathbf{e}_t &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{W}_d\mathbf{c}_t+\mathbf{b}_d).
  \end{split}
\end{equation}
The bottleneck is supervised with CLIP-derived top-$m$ pseudo-labels
($m=3$ in the main recipe), treated as a semantic bootstrap rather than
frame-level human ground truth. We then compute a global concept risk score
$\rho_t=\sigma(\mathbf{w}_{\mathrm{risk}})^\top\mathbf{c}_t$ and use
concept-delta temporal attention (\cgta{}) to focus history on semantic changes.
\tsd{} distills future CLIP features into current representations, and \caac{}
consumes the concept-augmented state for timing. In the current paper, actor
timing is support evidence; the primary claim is the governed semantic interface.

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We evaluate on DAD~\citep{ref4} and A3D~\citep{liao2024crash}. DAD contains
1,750 dashcam clips (620 accident, 1,130 non-accident) and stresses noisy,
short-horizon pre-crash transitions. A3D contains 1,199 accident videos and
serves as the cleaner flagship setting where precursor concepts emerge earlier.
Models use VGG16 ROI features~\citep{ref31}, AdamW, and matched launcher
families. We keep headline runs, controlled support analyses, and stress
evidence separate to avoid pooling heterogeneous checkpoints into a single
leaderboard narrative.

\paragraph{DAD robustness and hardening.}
The canonical DAD line is useful, but it is not the whole story. Three clean
curriculum seeds at synchronized checkpoints yield 56.39\%$\pm$3.46 AP and
2.40$\pm$0.05s mTTA at epoch 25, and 55.16\%$\pm$3.63 AP and
2.49$\pm$0.09s mTTA at epoch 30. A fixed-vocabulary recovery family improves
the stress envelope to 63.64\%$\pm$0.80 AP and 2.33$\pm$0.27s mTTA, while a
low-regularization follow-up reaches 64.42\%$\pm$0.97 AP and
2.35$\pm$0.20s mTTA. These runs narrow the gap, but they do not make DAD the
paper's clean mechanism dataset. We therefore report DAD as a meaningful stress
case and A3D as the clean flagship.

\paragraph{Language-side audits.}
The ontology is also audited as a language artifact. Over a 500-frame DAD slice,
top-1 pseudo-labeling is too concentrated, while top-10 is much more diffuse.
Top-3 produces 2.34 concept families per frame and 16.0\% of the cumulative
top-20 similarity mass, giving a practical balance between coverage and noise.
Across 12 canonical concepts with two paraphrases each, canonical and variant
prompts achieve mean text cosine 0.939, frame-score correlation 0.887, top-10
frame overlap 0.525, and mean absolute score difference 0.013. These numbers do
not make the ontology a finished human concept benchmark, but they support the
paper's naming policy: short canonical risk phrases are stable enough to act as
a governed interface.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why A3D carries the clean mechanism claim.}
The strongest mechanistic reading comes from A3D rather than DAD. A3D contains
longer and cleaner precursor intervals, so concept activations have room to
change before the warning threshold is crossed. In that regime, the discovered
ontology and the compact manual ontology occupy different points on the
AP--mTTA frontier, and actor-source diagnostics preserve much of the
classifier-side timing behavior. This is the evidence pattern expected if the
semantic interface is genuinely participating in anticipation rather than only
decorating a late binary score. By contrast, DAD is useful precisely because it
is less forgiving. Several DAD runs show that warning behavior can be sensitive
to checkpoint choice, trigger source, and vocabulary size. We therefore do not
use DAD to claim a clean causal timing mechanism. We use it to test whether a
language-grounded interface can remain interpretable under a short-horizon
stress setting while still producing a bounded, competitive operating point.

\paragraph{What the controlled comparisons rule out.}
The ontology comparisons rule out two easy explanations. First, the gain is not
explained by simply increasing the number of phrases: the 837-concept historical
vocabulary is not uniformly better than the compact or polished alternatives.
Second, the result is not a pure visualization layer: changing the ontology
while holding the launcher family fixed changes AP, mTTA, TTA@R80, and P@R80.
The correct interpretation is narrower and more useful. The ontology is a
modeling interface with measurable downstream effects, and different
interfaces favor different regions of the accuracy--timeliness frontier. This
also explains why the paper separates headline rows, ontology cells,
component ablations, actor-source diagnostics, and intervention analyses. A
single pooled table would make the package look stronger at first glance, but
would hide the distinction between direct predictive evidence, support
evidence, and stress evidence.

\paragraph{Reviewer-facing read of the contribution.}
\method{} should be read as semantic-interface methodology for anticipation,
not as a claim that language supervision alone solves accident prediction. The
paper contributes a reproducible path from multimodal phrase discovery to a
governed ontology, then evaluates that ontology as a trainable bottleneck
rather than as post-hoc prose. This framing gives reviewers three concrete
objects to inspect: the exported concept vocabulary, the matched ontology
experiments, and the concept-level audit traces attached to warning behavior.
It also keeps the remaining weaknesses visible. The concept labels are
pseudo-annotated, the ontology is not yet a human factors benchmark, and the
DAD actor branch is not strong enough to support a deployment-level timing
claim. Those limitations do not undercut the central result; they define the
next step from an auditable research interface to a fully validated
human-centered anticipation system.

\paragraph{How the interface can be falsified.}
The framework is useful only if the semantic layer can be challenged. A
post-hoc explanation can often be adjusted after a model has succeeded; a
governed bottleneck is less forgiving because the ontology is fixed before the
matched runs are compared. The current evidence exposes several falsification
routes. If a large uncurated phrase pool had dominated every compact ontology,
the governance story would be weak. If ontology swaps had left AP and timing
unchanged, the interface would be cosmetic. If concept edits had no measurable
effect on alert timing even in archived intervention tests, the bottleneck
would be difficult to interpret as a structural object. The observed results
are mixed in a productive way: ontology choice matters, A3D provides a clean
mechanism setting, and DAD reveals where the current actor-timing story breaks.
This is the kind of evidence profile we expect from an interface paper rather
than a pure architecture paper.

\paragraph{Practical scope.}
The method is intentionally compact: the reported \method{} configuration stays
near 30M parameters and uses offline ROI features, so the contribution is not
dependent on an unusually large video backbone. This matters for the paper's
scientific reading. The semantic interface can be swapped, audited, and
stress-tested without reinterpreting every result as a backbone scaling effect.
At the same time, the compact setting should not be mistaken for a deployment
recipe. Better end-to-end features, calibrated alert policies, and denser human
concept labels are natural next steps. The point of the current submission is
to make those future upgrades comparable: once the ontology is a first-class
artifact with provenance and matched evaluation cells, later work can improve
the perceptual front end without losing the ability to ask how semantic design
changes warning behavior.

\paragraph{Why governed concepts instead of captions alone.}
Free-form captions are attractive because they are expressive, but expression
alone does not create a stable experimental interface. Two captions can describe
the same road hazard with different surface forms, and a single caption can mix
actor identity, scene context, weather, and speculative intent in ways that are
hard to compare across runs. \method{} deliberately compresses language into
canonical risk primitives before the anticipation model sees it. This loses some
descriptive richness, but it gains three properties that matter for a scientific
paper: the vocabulary can be versioned, concept families can be balanced, and
downstream changes can be attributed to a known interface rather than to
uncontrolled prompt variation. The language contribution is therefore not that
the model says more words; it is that language defines a controlled semantic
state that can be trained, swapped, ablated, and audited.

\paragraph{What would make the claim stronger.}
The most valuable next experiment is not another unconstrained DAD leaderboard
run. It is a human-centered ontology validation pass that asks whether drivers,
safety engineers, and annotators agree that the canonical concepts are
semantically meaningful, separable, and useful for early warning review. A
second useful extension is to pair the current offline ROI features with a
modern video backbone while preserving the same ontology cells and audit
protocol. If the same frontier shifts persist under stronger features and
human-vetted concepts, the paper's methodological claim would become much
closer to a validated design principle for interpretable anticipation. The
current submission stops one step earlier: it establishes the interface, the
matched evaluation protocol, and the evidence that ontology design changes
model behavior.

\section{Conclusion}

\method{} reframes accident anticipation around a governed semantic interface.
The central contribution is not a new black-box leaderboard entry, but a
methodology for building, deploying, and evaluating language-grounded risk
concepts as a trainable model component. Empirically, the interface supports a
strong interpretable operating point, especially on A3D, and controlled ontology
comparisons show that semantic-interface design measurably changes downstream
behavior. The main remaining weakness is DAD-side mechanism fragility and the
gap between classifier-derived anticipation and mature policy-level timing.

\section*{Limitations}
The concept labels are based on CLIP-style pseudo-annotations rather than dense
human frame-level concept supervision. The current ontology is auditable and
reviewed, but not a finished human-validated concept benchmark. The evaluation
uses offline visual features rather than end-to-end video backbones, and
actor-policy timing remains support evidence rather than a complete causal
control account. Any deployment would require stronger calibration, robustness,
and human-factors validation.

\section*{Ethics and Reproducibility}
The paper studies offline accident anticipation and should not be read as a
deployment-ready ADAS component. The semantic interface can make model evidence
more inspectable, but it can also expose dataset bias when concepts are mined
from limited visual domains. We therefore keep the DAD stress evidence explicit,
avoid upgrading actor-policy timing diagnostics into deployment claims, and
treat concept names as auditable research artifacts rather than verified human
factors labels.

For reproducibility, the submission package separates headline checkpoints,
controlled ontology cells, support diagnostics, and stress evidence. The
ontology export retains canonical names, family metadata, merge provenance, and
review notes so that future work can replace the pseudo-labeled interface with
denser human concept supervision without changing the paper's evaluation
question.
